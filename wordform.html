🎯 What This Quiz Tests

This quiz tests your ability to choose and identify word forms — the different shapes a word can take depending on its role in a sentence. The four main parts of speech you need to know are:

  • Noun — names a person, place, thing, or idea
  • Verb — describes an action or state
  • Adjective — describes a noun
  • Adverb — describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb
📋 The 4 Question Types
  • Fill in the Blank: Choose the correct word form to complete a sentence. A base word is given.
  • Identify Part of Speech: A word in the sentence is highlighted — say whether it's a noun, verb, adjective, or adverb.
  • Word Transformation: Given a word and a target part of speech, pick the correct form (e.g. "strong" → noun = "strength").
  • Correct Usage: Four sentences are shown — only one uses the word form correctly.
🔑 The Key Skill: Recognising Suffixes

The most reliable way to identify or choose a word form is by its suffix (ending). Different parts of speech have different typical endings. Study the tabs for each part of speech to learn these patterns.

💡 Tip: When you see a blank in a sentence, first decide what part of speech is needed (noun? adjective? etc.), then choose the option with the matching suffix.
Noun What is a Noun?

A noun names a person, place, thing, or idea. In sentences, nouns typically act as the subject, object, or follow a preposition.

Example The artist's latest creation was exhibited at the museum.
Example Making a good decision requires careful thought.
🔤 Common Noun Suffixes

These endings signal that a word is a noun:

-tion / -sioncreation, decision
-mentachievement, development
-nesskindness, happiness
-ity / -tygenerosity, curiosity
-ance / -enceattendance, difference
-er / -orexplorer, inventor
-alsurvival, arrival
-dom / -thfreedom, warmth
📐 Where Nouns Appear
  • After "the", "a", "an", "my", "his": The protection of animals…
  • After adjectives: a remarkable achievement
  • As the subject: Pollution is harming our planet.
  • After "of", "for", "in", "with": in conclusion, with permission
  • After "a/an" + adjective: a vivid imagination
💡 Tip: If a blank comes after "the", "a/an", an adjective, or a preposition, you almost certainly need a noun.
Verb What is a Verb?

A verb describes an action (run, create) or a state (is, seems). Verbs are the engine of every sentence.

Example The students discussed the topic in class.
Example Scientists classify animals into groups.
🔤 Common Verb Suffixes

These endings often signal that a word is a verb:

-ifysimplify, purify, classify
-ize / -isememorize, apologize
-enstrengthen, widen
-atecommunicate, educate
📐 Where Verbs Appear
  • After "to": We need to widen the road.
  • After "can/could/will/should": Exercise can strengthen your muscles.
  • After the subject: The committee approved the plan.
  • After "did/does/do": She did not hesitate.
💡 Tip: If a blank comes after "to", a modal verb (can, will, must, should), or a subject pronoun, you likely need a verb.
Adjective What is an Adjective?

An adjective describes or modifies a noun. It tells you what something is like — its size, colour, quality, etc.

Example She was extremely successful in passing all her exams.
Example She has a very generous personality.
🔤 Common Adjective Suffixes

These endings often signal that a word is an adjective:

-fulbeautiful, helpful
-ous / -iousdangerous, generous
-ivecreative, attractive
-able / -iblereliable, comfortable
-altraditional, natural
-ent / -antindependent, hesitant
📐 Where Adjectives Appear
  • Before a noun: a remarkable comeback
  • After "is/was/are/were/seems/looks/feels": The painting was valuable.
  • After "very/extremely/quite/really": very dangerous
💡 Tip: If a blank is between "very" and nothing else, or between an article and a noun, you need an adjective. Also, after linking verbs like "is", "was", "looks", "feels", "seems" — use an adjective, NOT an adverb.
Adverb What is an Adverb?

An adverb modifies a verb, an adjective, or another adverb. It tells you how, when, where, or to what extent something happens.

Example She spoke confidently during the debate.
Example The journalist reported the facts accurately.
🔤 Common Adverb Suffixes

Most adverbs are formed by adding -ly to the adjective:

-lycarefully, beautifully
-ouslycourageously, dangerously
-ivelydecisively, creatively
-allyscientifically, professionally
📐 Where Adverbs Appear
  • Before or after a verb: He carefully opened the book. / He acted courageously.
  • Before an adjective: The view was incredibly beautiful.
  • At the start of a sentence: Fortunately, nobody was injured.
  • Before another adverb: She ran extremely quickly.
💡 Tip: If a word describes how an action is done (modifies a verb), you need an adverb. A common mistake is using an adjective after an action verb — remember: "He acted courageously" (NOT "courageous").
⚠️ Adjective vs Adverb — The Big Trap

Many students confuse adjectives and adverbs. The key rule:

  • Linking verbs (is, was, looks, feels, seems, sounds) → use an adjective: The garden looked beautiful.
  • Action verbs (ran, spoke, played, acted) → use an adverb: She spoke beautifully.
Adjective (linking verb) The village was very peaceful. ✓
Adverb (action verb) She left the room very quietly. ✓

🧠 Step-by-Step Strategy

Follow these steps for every question:

  • Step 1: Read the whole sentence first.
  • Step 2: Identify what part of speech the blank/question needs (noun, verb, adjective, adverb).
  • Step 3: Look at the words around the blank for clues (articles, prepositions, verbs).
  • Step 4: Check the suffix of each option to match the required part of speech.
  • Step 5: Read the full sentence with your answer to check it makes sense.

⚡ Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Using an adjective instead of an adverb after action verbs: ✗ "She spoke confident" → ✓ "She spoke confidently"
  • Using an adverb instead of an adjective after linking verbs: ✗ "It looks beautifully" → ✓ "It looks beautiful"
  • Confusing -tion (noun) with -tive (adjective): "His imagination is great" (noun needed) vs. "He is imaginative" (adjective needed)
  • Forgetting noun suffixes like -ment, -ness, -ity: "Her kindness made everyone feel welcome" — NOT "kind" or "kindly"
  • Mixing up -er (person) with -tion (thing/concept): "The explorer discovered a cave" (person) vs. "The exploration took years" (concept)
